Book Review

A book titled An American Sickness: How Healthcare Became Big Business and How You Can Take It Back written by Elisabeth Rosenthal covers the serious problems or “illnesses” of the American healthcare system. This book is largely divided into two parts: (1) the history of the present illness and review of systems; and (2) diagnosis and treatment: prescriptions for taking back our healthcare. The author describes the current status of overall healthcare system such as insurance, hospitals, pharmaceuticals, medical resources, and medical business and its pitfalls in Part ?, and suggests several strategies for avoiding the huge bills of the healthcare system in Part ?. After reading An American Sickness, I feel anger at how the healthcare system is pursuing the profit rather than patients’ happiness or well-being and disappointment at the current medical market where human beings became a means not a purpose. Besides, it seems to me that the spirit of respect for life in Hippocrates has been forgotten by physicians in modern societies. In this paper, I will tackle the unfair medical billing issue and discuss what nurse practitioners can do to protect patients from it.Firstly, the evidence-based medicine (EBM) is being exploited as one of the profit- seeking methods of the medical market. To buttress my statement, I will quote the statement from Rosenthal’s economic rules of the dysfunctional medical market: “Rule 1: More treatment is always better. Default to the most expensive option.” (Rosenthal, 2017, p. 8). On page 201, the author criticizes the loophole of EBM and its guidelines in practice. For instance, Mohs surgery, as one of treatment packages in dermatology which includes a scraping and application of a cream for removing scars, exceeds the price of usual treatments even if it has little evidence to suggest it in general cases. In fact, the EBM is not “cook book” with recipes, however, its application brings cost-effective and better healthcare. To understand this ironic situation, the author points out that the specialists who make money from procedures create the guidelines for when and how often they should be performed. In other words, it is a reality that the stakeholders of medical guidelines mainly are involved in the development of medical guidelines. On page 210, Dr. Lakowsky confessed that he felt heavy pressure to refer patients for testing and surgery and to recommend expensive treatments within the Sutter system, which was overpriced. At this juncture, who can be patients’ advocate instead of physicians? Secondly, the influence of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) on the medical market is doubtful. On page 237, Rosenthal states that hospitals and doctors are undermining the philosophy of ACA. The ACA also called Obamacare, was enacted by the 111th United States Congress in 2010 to protect every single American from the expenses of American medicine, and to relieve the profit-oriented medical system. However, unfortunately, hospitals and doctors are pursuing profits on the blind side of the ACA. To explain this problem, the case of a colonoscopy has to be mentioned here. According to the ACA, individuals who visit the endoscopic center for a free screening procedure don’t have to pay any bill. Meanwhile, if a polyp was removed during the screening unexpectedly, the patients have to pay for the treatment. In this gap, hospitals and doctors charge aggressive billing to patients to compensate for the small financial incentives of the ACA. Despite the enactment of ACA, still many Americans are afraid of seeing a doctor due to the unaffordable medical cost. From now on, I will discuss how nurse practitioners can protect the vulnerable populations in healthcare from the tyranny of this medical market in light of An American Sickness. Regarding the EBM issue, nurse practitioners should be involved in the development of treatment guidelines to ensure the transparency of the overall process and to verify its cost effectiveness systematically. According to Thomas and Courtney (2018), when nurse practitioners were involved in the development of clinical guidelines related to the child obesity management, statistically significant results were obtained regarding guideline adherence, and the parents’ satisfaction with medical service was higher than before. Thomas and Courtney (2018) stated that the DNP investigator in this project was in a unique role to creatively translate evidence-based guidelines into practice. Here is another example. McCrory, Patton, Moore, O’Connor, and Nugent (2018) demonstrated the fact that the intervention of a nurse practitioner working collaboratively with a physician can surpass the health-related outcomes comparing to the general care and can reduce the medical costs meaningfully. Lastly, nurse practitioners can serve as a guide for patients’ care plan and medical expenses. At the end of An American Sickness, the author provides several tips for ordinary people to cope with the huge bills from the hospitals. Although she does not offer fundamental solutions for the unaffordable healthcare in the United States, these practical advices can be helpful to readers. Some of the advice is to make sure that the specific treatment is necessary for themselves and to see if they should purchase the prescribed medical equipment when they discharge (Rosenthal, 2017, p. 265). Actually, patients have a lack of knowledge in healthcare and they are not well-informed about their care plan in the future. Therefore, there is a need for a specialist who can consult with patients about treatment guidelines and medical expenses. Without a doubt, only a nurse practitioner who monitors and cares for patients near the bedside can perform this role effectively. Of course, this role of nurse practitioners should be independent from the hospitals and doctors. In sum, An American Sickness allows healthcare providers to be alert to the industrialized medical market and to have doubts on the medical bills. Although Rosenthal doesn’t mention the nurses’ roles in her book, nurse practitioners should be actively involved in the“holistic” care for patients’ well-being.
